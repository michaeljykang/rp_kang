Background

Planarians are among the earliest organisms to evolve a brain, enabling learning and memory. Although their capacity for memory has been debated, numerous studies confirm that planarians possess long-term memory lasting several days. Light can be an effective stimulus for training, and planaria provide valuable insights into human behavior. Experiments have shown they can be trained to develop preferences through conditioning. Planarians also withstand starvation by shrinking while maintaining body proportions, including constant ratios of brain and visual neurons. Their nervous system contains all classical neurotransmitters, and unlike most animals, they can sustain or increase metabolism during food deprivation.

Rationale

This study investigates how starvation influences planarian memory, aiming to clarify how limited resources affect cognitive functions. Starvationâ€™s effects on memory have been observed in other species, such as fruit flies, where long-term memory formation declines while short-term memory persists, though mild deprivation can sometimes enhance long-term memory. By examining how starvation alters both the accuracy and speed of trained responses, this research may reveal broader principles about the relationship between nutrition, brain function, and memory in both simple and complex organisms.
